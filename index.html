This is how I think hackers steal from end users

In this blog, imagine I am the hacker and Mr. Bob is the victim. Bob banks with PhishBank, a new bank in town. He loves PhishBank because of their excellent online services. Bob has a strong, secure password (he doesn’t share it with anyone) and a lot of money in his account.

Since PhishBank is new, they roll out constant updates. Naturally, they send Bob emails about new features and upgrades. Bob trusts these emails because… well, PhishBank is a great bank, right?

Now here’s where I step in. I want Bob’s money — a lot of it. But I’m wary of those cybersecurity folks with their dozens of certificates lurking around.

“Instead of attacking the fortress, why not trick the man holding the keys?”

So, I try a different approach: targeting Bob directly. I register a domain name that looks almost like PhishBank’s. Then, I send him an email:

“Good day Mr. Bob, we have a new update on the system. Please log in within the next hour to avoid losing access.”

Bob panics. Afraid of losing his wealth, he rushes to the provided login link — not realizing it’s my fake site. He enters his username and password. Seconds later, he receives an OTP SMS from the real PhishBank. He dutifully types it in. Everything looks normal… or does it?

Here’s the truth: Bob was never on the real site. He handed his login credentials directly to me.

My fake login page captured his details and stored them in my database. A script I wrote even forwarded the credentials to the real PhishBank system to keep things looking legit — that way, Bob still gets his OTP and thinks all is fine.

And just like that…
I AM IN!!!
